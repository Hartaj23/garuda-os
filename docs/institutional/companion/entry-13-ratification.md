---

## Definition

**Ratification** is the formal institutional act through which a completed governance proposal is accepted into the authoritative corpus of Project Garuda.

Ratification transforms a proposal into institutional authority.

Before ratification, a governance artifact remains a draft.
After ratification, it becomes part of the institution's enduring constitutional, governance, or other authorized institutional record, according to its intended role.

Ratification therefore marks the transition from authorship to institutional legitimacy.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of ratification is to preserve the distinction between creating a proposal and adopting institutional authority.

Drafting demonstrates what is possible.
Ratification determines what the institution accepts.

This separation protects the institution from confusing careful work with legitimate authority.

---

## Position Within the Governance Lifecycle

Ratification occurs only after:

- appropriate authorization,
- completion of the authorized draft,
- institutional review.

```text
Authorization
       ↓
Draft
       ↓
Review
       ↓
Ratification
       ↓
Institutional Authority
```

No proposal becomes authoritative without this explicit transition.

---

## Characteristics

### Deliberate

Ratification is an intentional institutional decision.

Authority is created through explicit acceptance rather than by the passage of time or the quality of the draft alone.

### Transformative

Ratification changes the institutional status of an artifact.

It does not merely acknowledge completion.
It establishes legitimacy.

### Traceable

Every ratification is recorded within the repository.

Future stewards can identify:

- what was ratified,
- when it became authoritative,
- the governance process through which it reached that state.

### Independent

Ratification remains distinct from:

- drafting,
- architectural interpretation,
- sprint authorization,
- engineering implementation.

Acceptance of one stage does not automatically authorize the next.

## Common Misunderstandings

### "Completing a draft makes it authoritative."

Incorrect.

Completion demonstrates readiness for review.
Only ratification creates institutional authority.

### "Ratification automatically authorizes downstream work."

No.

Ratification establishes the status of the artifact itself.

Subsequent architectural, sprint, or engineering work requires its own explicit authorization.

### "Ratification means the artifact can never change."

No.

Future governance cycles may amend, refine, or replace institutional authority through the same disciplined governance process.

Ratification establishes the current institutional baseline.
It does not prevent future constitutional evolution.

## Reading This Concept

Ratification should be understood as the institution's act of adoption.

It marks the moment when responsibility shifts from the author of a proposal to the institution itself.

The artifact no longer represents an individual's reasoning alone.
It becomes part of the institution's shared constitutional and historical inheritance.

---

## Key Insight

Ratification is the moment at which institutional legitimacy becomes permanent enough to guide future generations.

It does not celebrate the completion of a document.
It recognizes the institution's decision to accept responsibility for that document as part of its own enduring identity.

In Project Garuda, that transition is never implied.
It is recorded explicitly, preserved permanently, and remembered as one of the defining acts of Constitutional Engineering.

Through ratification, ideas cease to belong solely to their authors.
They become part of the institution they were created to serve.
