---

## Definition

**Gate B** is the second formal decision point within a Governance Cycle.

It is the Founder Authorization that determines whether drafting of a specifically recommended governance artifact may begin.

Gate B concerns authorization only.

It does not determine constitutional necessity, approve the contents of a draft, or establish constitutional authority.

Its purpose is to grant or withhold permission to begin the authorized governance work.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of Gate B is to preserve the distinction between institutional direction and institutional action.

A positive Gate A decision establishes that constitutional evolution should be considered.
An Institutional Recommendation identifies the appropriate governance artifact.

Gate B then determines whether the institution should commit resources to drafting that artifact.

By separating these responsibilities, Project Garuda ensures that no recommendation becomes implied authorization.

---

## The Gate B Question

Every recommendation ultimately asks one constitutional question.

```text
May drafting of the recommended
governance artifact begin?
```

Only two legitimate answers exist.

```text
           YES
            │
            ▼
Governance Draft
──────────────
            NO
            │
            ▼
Institutional HOLD
```

Authorization is explicit.

Silence is not authorization.
Expectation is not authorization.
Recommendation is not authorization.

---

## Characteristics

### Founder Authorization

Gate B is exercised through explicit Founder authorization.

It records permission to begin a defined governance activity.

### Artifact-Specific

Gate B authorizes drafting of a specific governance artifact.

It does not authorize unrelated governance work.

Authority remains precisely scoped.

### Limited Scope

Gate B authorizes drafting only.

It does not authorize:

- constitutional adoption,
- architectural interpretation,
- sprint planning,
- engineering implementation.

Each subsequent activity requires its own explicit institutional authority.

### Traceable

Every Gate B authorization becomes part of the Governance Corpus.

Future stewards can reconstruct when drafting began, what was authorized, and under what institutional circumstances.

## Common Misunderstandings

### "Gate B approves the constitutional proposal."

Incorrect.

Gate B authorizes preparation of the proposal.
Approval occurs only through Founder ratification after drafting has been completed.

### "Gate B follows automatically after a recommendation."

No.

Institutional Recommendation and Founder Authorization are separate governance acts.

A recommendation may exist without subsequent authorization.

### "Gate B authorizes future governance work."

No.

Gate B is intentionally narrow.

Its authority extends only to the specifically identified governance artifact.

Further constitutional, architectural, sprint, or engineering work requires separate institutional authorization.

## Repository References

Gate B is documented within the Governance Corpus through Founder Authorization records, Governance Cycle documentation, the Two-Gate Model, and related institutional governance artifacts.

These records illustrate the distinction between recommending constitutional work and authorizing it.

---

## Reading This Concept

Gate B should be understood as the institution's deliberate pause between intention and execution.

Rather than allowing institutional momentum to carry recommendations directly into drafting, Project Garuda requires an additional act of explicit constitutional authorization.

This preserves clarity, accountability, and disciplined governance.

---

## Key Insight

Gate B demonstrates that institutional readiness is not the same as institutional permission.

Even after the institution has concluded that constitutional evolution should be explored, it pauses once more before beginning the work itself.

That pause is intentional.

It reminds every future steward that constitutional authority is never inherited through expectation.
It is granted explicitly, exercised carefully, and preserved transparently.

In doing so, Gate B transforms authorization from a procedural step into an enduring institutional safeguard.
