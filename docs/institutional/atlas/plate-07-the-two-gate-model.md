## Purpose

This plate illustrates the Two-Gate Model established during Governance Cycle 6.

The model separates institutional direction from authorization to begin work.

This distinction preserves explicit Founder authority at every major governance transition.

---

# The Two-Gate Flow

```text
             Institutional Assessment
                      │
                      ▼
          Gate A — Founder Decision
   Is constitutional evolution required?
          ┌──────────┴──────────┐
          │                     │
          ▼                     ▼
         NO                    YES
          │                     │
          ▼                     ▼
Institutional HOLD     Institutional Recommendation
                       Identify governance artifact
                               │
                               ▼
         Gate B — Founder Authorization
      May drafting of this artifact begin?
          ┌──────────┴──────────┐
          │                     │
          ▼                     ▼
         NO                    YES
          │                     │
          ▼                     ▼
Institutional HOLD     Governance Draft
                               │
                               ▼
                    Founder Ratification
                               │
                               ▼
                     Governance Closure
                               │
                               ▼
                    Institutional HOLD
```

---

# The Two Questions

The Two-Gate Model deliberately separates two different institutional questions.

## Gate A

**Question**

Should the institution evolve?

**Purpose**

Determine whether constitutional evolution is necessary.

**Authority**

Founder Decision.

**Effect**

Provides institutional direction only.
It does **not** authorize drafting.

## Gate B

**Question**

May drafting begin?

**Purpose**

Authorize preparation of a specific governance artifact.

**Authority**

Founder Authorization.

**Effect**

Authorizes drafting only.
It does **not** ratify the proposal.

---

# Why Two Gates?

Without separation, several distinct governance acts become blurred:

- deciding,
- recommending,
- authorizing,
- drafting,
- ratifying.

The Two-Gate Model preserves each as an independent institutional event.

This improves:

- auditability,
- traceability,
- clarity of authority,
- historical accuracy.

---

# Governance Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Decision | What the Founder concludes |
| Recommendation | What institutional assessment proposes |
| Authorization | Permission to begin work |
| Draft | A proposed governance artifact |
| Ratification | Formal acceptance into the institutional corpus |

Each term represents a different governance responsibility.
None should be used interchangeably.

---

# Example Sequence

```text
Assessment
      ↓
Gate A
      ↓
Recommendation
      ↓
Gate B
      ↓
Draft
      ↓
Ratification
```

Notice that authorization follows recommendation and precedes drafting.

This ordering preserves explicit institutional intent.

---

# Institutional Benefits

The Two-Gate Model ensures that:

- recommendations never become implied authorization,
- drafting never becomes implied ratification,
- governance remains transparent,
- future reviewers can distinguish every transition.

The authority chain remains explicit from beginning to end.

---

# Relationship to the Governance Cycle

The Two-Gate Model is not a separate governance process.
It is a refinement within the Governance Cycle.

Gate A evaluates institutional necessity.
Gate B authorizes institutional work.

Together they strengthen governance without increasing unnecessary complexity.

---

# Reading This Plate

Every governance artifact created after adoption of the Two-Gate Model can be understood through these questions:

1. Has the institution decided that change is required?
2. Has the Founder authorized work to begin?
3. Has drafting occurred?
4. Has the proposal been ratified?

If any answer is "no," the authority chain is incomplete.

---

# Key Insight

The Two-Gate Model demonstrates that institutional direction and institutional authorization are different acts of governance.

Knowing **what should happen** is not the same as granting permission **to begin making it happen**.

By preserving that distinction, Project Garuda ensures that every meaningful governance transition remains deliberate, explicit, and fully traceable.
