## The Question

Phase A left one question for reflection:

*"If someone had never seen Garuda before, would this architecture naturally teach them how to think?"*

The refined question:

**"Does the website invite curiosity before it asks for understanding?"**

People will not begin with Constitutional Engineering.

They will begin with curiosity.

The architecture should gently lead them from:

1. *"I've never heard of this."*
2. *"This is interesting."*
3. *"I understand the philosophy."*
4. *"I want to read the constitutions."*

That progression is what Phase A has made possible.

## Recommendation Before Phase B

During the reflection period:

- Do not open Cursor.
- Do not edit Markdown.
- Do not sketch logos.

Do something surprisingly simple:

Open the navigation.

Read it from top to bottom.

Pretend you are:

1. A university professor
2. A software engineer
3. A policymaker
4. A student
5. Someone who knows nothing about AI governance

Ask only one question each time:

**"Where would I click next?"**

If the answer feels natural across all five perspectives, Phase A has truly succeeded.

## Phase B — Visual Philosophy (Intent Only)

Phase B is **not authorized**.

When it is authorized, branding should be approached differently from most organizations.

Most companies create a logo.

Garuda should first define its **visual philosophy**.

Only then should a logo emerge.

The visual philosophy should communicate:

- calm,
- clarity,
- permanence,
- legitimacy,
- stewardship.

Not futuristic.

Not corporate.

Not "AI."

The visual identity should feel like a **university press met a constitutional archive**.

Timeless rather than trendy.
