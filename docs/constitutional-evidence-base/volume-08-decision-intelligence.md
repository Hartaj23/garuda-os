## Inquiry 1 — What Is Decision Intelligence?

### The Question

What must an intelligence do — beyond predicting or retrieving — to count as decision-capable under uncertainty?

### Investigation

Decision intelligence is the disciplined capacity to transform incomplete inputs into justified commitments (or justified refusals) under uncertainty, subject to constraints of the environment, the agent, and finite stakes.

Prediction alone is not decision intelligence (Volume VI process vs prediction). Retrieval alone is not. Persuasive narrative alone is not (Volume II). Decision intelligence includes: framing the question, gathering and weighing evidence, stating uncertainty, considering alternatives and failure modes, sizing consequences, and binding action — or refusing to act — under process.

Investing is an especially hard test: noisy feedback, adversarial structure, social contagion, and ruin.

### Established Observations

- High predictive score can coexist with poor decision process.
- Agents and systems often skip from data to recommendation without auditable intermediate steps.
- Refusal and deferral are decision competencies, not absences of intelligence.

### Competing Perspectives

- Intelligence is primarily prediction vs. primarily judgment under values and constraints.
- Explicit decision pipelines improve outcomes vs. expert intuition resists decomposition.
- Automation should decide vs. only advise — enduring institutional disagreement.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Decision intelligence is the transformation of evidence into justified commitment or refusal — not prediction alone.
- An investment intelligence that cannot show its path from evidence to act (or refusal) is incomplete.

## Inquiry 2 — Information, Evidence, Knowledge, Judgment, Decision

### The Question

How do these five relate — and what goes wrong when they are conflated?

### Investigation

Without freezing final definitions, the CEB records a working separation forced by prior volumes:

| Term | Working relationship (descriptive) |
| --- | --- |
| **Information** | Raw or structured signals available to an agent — prices, filings, news, sensory data — not yet validated for a claim (Volumes I, V) |
| **Evidence** | Information selected, contextualized, and weighed as bearing on a specific question — including conflicting evidence (CEB-0000; Volumes I–IV) |
| **Knowledge** | Relatively stable, cross-checked understanding that survives scrutiny better than a single evidence snapshot — still fallible and revisable (Reversibility; Volumes III–IV) |
| **Judgment** | The weighing of knowledge, uncertainty, values, and constraints toward what *ought* to be done or believed in a case — not entailed by data alone (Volumes II, VI) |
| **Decision** | A commitment of scarce resources (or refusal) that closes options under uncertainty (Volumes VI–VII) |

Common failure modes:

- Treating information as evidence (every tick as proof)
- Treating evidence as knowledge (one study as settled truth)
- Treating knowledge as judgment (facts dictate acts without stakes or values)
- Treating judgment as decision (opinion without commitment)
- Treating decision as vindicated solely by outcome (Volume VI–VII watchpoint)

### Established Observations

- Conflation of these layers produces false certainty and un-auditable advice.
- Professional domains that handle life-critical risk (medicine, aviation) separate data, evidence, and decision more carefully than retail market culture often does.
- CEB methodology itself instantiates information→evidence discipline before constitutional knowledge claims.

### Competing Perspectives

- The five-layer split is philosophically necessary vs. over-fine for practical systems.
- Knowledge is justified true belief vs. knowledge is reliable process output (epistemology debates).
- Judgment is irreducible human vs. partially formalizable under explicit values.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Information ≠ evidence ≠ knowledge ≠ judgment ≠ decision — conflation is a constitutional hazard for intelligence systems.
- Investment intelligence must make transitions between these layers explicit enough to audit.

## Inquiry 3 — What Makes a Decision Justified?

### The Question

When is a decision warranted by evidence — and when is it merely motivated by narrative, incentive, or hope?

### Investigation

Justification is not certainty. It is the property that a decision (or refusal) can be defended by: relevant evidence considered, material counterevidence not ignored, uncertainty acknowledged, alternatives weighed, stakes and failure modes sized, and process constraints respected.

Volume VI decision quality vs outcome says justification is graded by process, not by whether the trade won. Volume VII says justification is purpose-relative to preservation and objectives.

Unjustified decisions can win. Justified decisions can lose. That is the probabilistic environment.

### Established Observations

- Justification requires engagement with contrary evidence, not only confirming stories (Volume II; Evidence Integrity).
- Stakes change the threshold of justification (medical analogy; ruin analogy).
- Incentives can produce motivated reasoning that mimics justification (Volume III).

### Competing Perspectives

- Justification is primarily Bayesian updating vs. primarily procedural/checklist vs. primarily virtue of the judge.
- Markets make ex ante justification unverifiable vs. process audit remains meaningful.
- Client consent substitutes for justification vs. consent without understanding is insufficient.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Justified decision ≠ successful outcome; justified decision ≠ confident tone.
- Investment intelligence must separate warrant from wish and from after-the-fact luck.

## Inquiry 4 — How Should Evidence Be Weighed Under Conflict?

### The Question

What enduring properties govern intelligent response when evidence disagrees?

### Investigation

Conflict is normal: bullish price action vs weak cash conversion (Volumes IV–V); expert disagreement (Volume I schools); macro vs micro (Volume III–IV). Decision intelligence that resolves conflict by silencing minority evidence violates Evidence Integrity (CEB-0000). Intelligence that never resolves enough to act violates stewardship of finite opportunity (Volume VII).

Enduring moves: identify the question; classify evidence type; note dependence (correlated sources are not independent); state what would change one's mind; decide, defer, or refuse.

### Established Observations

- More sources do not equal more independent evidence.
- Conflict often signals uncertainty to be preserved, not error to be erased.
- Premature consensus is a social phenomenon (Volume II) as much as an epistemic one.

### Competing Perspectives

- Always wait for convergence vs. act under residual conflict with smaller size.
- Meta-analysis / markets as aggregators resolve conflict vs. aggregators share biases.
- Explicit scoring of evidence quality helps vs. false precision in weighing.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Decision intelligence must preserve material conflict in its reasoning trail — not only in its private doubt.
- Resolution of conflict is a decision stage, not a background cleanup of the record.

## Inquiry 5 — What Is the Role of Uncertainty in Justified Decision?

### The Question

How should acknowledged unknownness enter the decision — rather than being deleted before the recommendation?

### Investigation

Volumes I and VI established risk vs uncertainty and confidence vs certainty. Decision intelligence fails when it compresses uncertainty into a single label ("Buy") without remainder. It also fails when it uses uncertainty as universal refusal while capital and time still demand stewardship.

Justified decisions **carry** uncertainty: in language, in size, in contingencies, in monitoring triggers, and in refusal thresholds. Uncertainty is an input to the decision function — not a shameful residue.

### Established Observations

- Suppressing uncertainty increases expressed confidence without increasing knowledge (Volume II).
- Size and refusal are primary vehicles for respecting uncertainty (Volumes VI–VII).
- Users often demand certainty the environment cannot supply; systems can collude or resist.

### Competing Perspectives

- Quantify all uncertainty vs. some uncertainty should remain qualitative.
- Always show confidence intervals vs. intervals often misread as certainty bands.
- The honest system frustrates users vs. education can align expectations.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Uncertainty must appear in the decision artifact — not only in the research scrap.
- An intelligence that deletes uncertainty to sound decisive fails justification under probabilistic environments.

## Inquiry 6 — When Must Intelligence Refuse?

### The Question

What enduring conditions make non-recommendation the justified act?

### Investigation

Refusal is a first-class decision (Volume VI process). Triggers recur across domains: insufficient evidence for the question asked; undefined objective or risk budget (Volume VII); structural prices untrustworthy (Volume V); category error in the question (value vs quotation); conflict of incentive; user request for certainty the system cannot warrant; action that would violate preservation priors.

Refusal is not ignorance theater. It is **justified non-commitment** with reasons and, where possible, what evidence would lift the refusal.

### Established Observations

- Systems that cannot refuse become engines of overclaim.
- Professionals in high-stakes fields treat refusal/escalation as competence.
- "I don't know" without structure is incomplete; refusal with criteria is teachable.

### Competing Perspectives

- Product success requires always answering vs. trust requires selective silence.
- Refusal should be rare and dramatic vs. routine and calm.
- Refuse the act but still provide education vs. refuse both when question is malformed.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Refusal is part of decision intelligence — not a failure of the product.
- Justified refusal states why, what is missing, and what would change the stance.

## Inquiry 7 — How Do Values and Objectives Enter Without Becoming Hidden Bias?

### The Question

If judgment is not entailed by evidence alone, how do purposes shape decisions legitimately?

### Investigation

Volume VII made portfolios purpose-relative. Two agents with the same evidence may decide differently given liabilities, horizons, and preservation types. That is not always bias; it is sometimes legitimate value-conditioning of judgment.

Illicit bias: undisclosed incentives, unexamined framing, narrative capture (Volume II). Licit conditioning: explicit objectives, risk budgets, constraints. Decision intelligence must **surface** the value layer rather than smuggle it inside "the data says."

### Established Observations

- Evidence underdetermines action when objectives differ.
- Hidden objectives produce advice that feels objective but isn't.
- Mandate clarity improves auditability of judgment.

### Competing Perspectives

- Systems should remain value-neutral vs. neutrality is itself a value choice.
- Elicit user values explicitly vs. infer from behaviour (revealed preference).
- Fiduciary-style duties can be encoded vs. remain irreducibly institutional/human.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Judgment joins evidence to explicit objectives; hiding the join is a justification failure.
- Investment intelligence should separate "what the evidence supports" from "what your objective warrants."

## Inquiry 8 — How Should Process Bind Prediction?

### The Question

What enduring relationship should process and prediction have inside decision intelligence?

### Investigation

The watched distinction **process vs prediction** (Volumes VI–VII) becomes identity-defining here. Prediction proposes states of the world. Process governs what will be done across states: evidence standards, size rules, refusal rules, review cadence, and update triggers.

An intelligence optimized only for prediction will speak fluently and fail under surprise. An intelligence with process and no evidence discipline will be rigid theater. Decision intelligence requires **prediction subordinated to process** — forecasts as inputs, not as sovereign commands.

### Established Observations

- Forecast error is normal; process for error is optional only in careless systems.
- Precommitment improves behaviour under arousal (Volume II).
- Updating process after every noisy outcome destroys process (feedback cadence — Volume VI).

### Competing Perspectives

- Learn continuously by revising process vs. freeze process to measure it.
- Prediction-first architectures with thin process wrappers suffice vs. process-first architectures with prediction as module.
- Discretion to override process is expertise vs. the usual death of process.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Process must bind prediction when stakes are consequential under uncertainty.
- Dr. Garuda's philosophical identity, if earned later, would be process-disciplined decision support — not raw prediction supremacy. (Watchpoint — not elevated.)

## Inquiry 9 — How Should Outcomes Train — Without Corrupting — Decision Intelligence?

### The Question

How can systems learn from results without collapsing into outcome-worship?

### Investigation

The watched distinction **decision quality vs outcome** (Volumes VI–VII) is the training hazard. Outcomes are information (Volume VIII Inquiry 2) — sometimes weak evidence about process quality, often weak because of variance, regime shift, and sample size.

Justified learning: compare outcome to what the decision process claimed; update calibration; seek base rates; avoid rewarding hidden risk that merely has not exploded. Unjustified learning: "we made money, so the reasoning was good."

### Established Observations

- Outcome feedback is necessary and dangerous.
- Short samples mis-train both humans and machines.
- Process audits plus outcomes beat outcomes alone for improving decision systems.

### Competing Perspectives

- End wealth is the only score that matters vs. process scores are required for non-ergodic agents.
- Markets eventually reveal truth vs. survivors' truths dominate the archive.
- Automated outcome-based training is scalable wisdom vs. scalable overfitting.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Outcomes may inform calibration; they must not solely define decision quality.
- Investment intelligence should train on process-adherence and calibration — not on P&L theater alone.

## Inquiry 10 — What Is Explainability — as Justification, Not Theater?

### The Question

What must an explanation contain to support justified decision — rather than to soothe?

### Investigation

Explainability in decision intelligence is the capacity to exhibit the path: question → information considered → evidence weighed → uncertainties → objectives → judgment → decision/refusal → monitoring triggers.

Cosmetic explainability: plausible stories after the fact (Volume II narrative). Justificatory explainability: counterevidence, alternatives, and failure modes included. Volume IV enterprise vs quotation and Volume V signal vs noise must appear when relevant — or the explanation is category-confused.

### Established Observations

- Humans demand reasons; markets punish shallow reasons slowly or suddenly.
- Post-hoc narratives are cheap; pre-decision audit trails are costly and valuable.
- Explanations that omit uncertainty and conflict train false trust.

### Competing Perspectives

- Full transparency is mandatory vs. overwhelming users harms decisions.
- Natural-language stories help vs. structured artifacts help more for audit.
- Model feature attributions equal explanation vs. often miss objectives and constraints.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Explanation is part of justification — it must expose conflict and uncertainty, not only supporting colour.
- An intelligence that cannot exhibit why it refused or acted fails decision-intelligence standards.

## Inquiry 12 — What Must Investment Intelligence Assume Before It Speaks a Decision?

### The Question

What defaults make a recommendation (or refusal) constitutionally complete as decision support?

### Investigation

Descriptive defaults (not law):

1. State the question being answered
2. Separate information from evidence used
3. Surface material conflicting evidence
4. Declare uncertainty without deleting it
5. Separate knowledge claims from case judgment
6. Make objectives/preservation priors explicit
7. Show the path to decision *or* refusal
8. Include size/risk/failure modes when action is advised (Volumes VI–VII)
9. Bind prediction with process and monitoring triggers
10. Forbid certainty language about open futures
11. Grade later learning by process and calibration — not outcome alone
12. Explain in justificatory form — including what would change the mind

### Established Observations

- Directional utterances without this stack are speech acts that impersonate decisions.
- Users experience advice as decisions even when disclaimers say otherwise — design must respect that psychological fact (Volume II).

### Competing Perspectives

- Minimum viable advice should be short vs. incomplete short advice is harmful.
- Professionals need the full stack; consumers need a subset vs. both need the same epistemic virtues at different resolutions.
- Legal disclaimers substitute for justification vs. legal text without epistemic process is theater.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Completeness of decision intelligence is measured by the evidence-to-decision path — not by eloquence or predicted alpha.
- An investment intelligence worthy of trust earns justified belief before it asks for consequential action.

5. Uncertainty must be carried into the decision artifact.
