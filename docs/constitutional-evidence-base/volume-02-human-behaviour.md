## Prefatory Note

Volume I asked what must be true of markets.
Volume II asks what must be true of the humans who act inside them.

Markets clear through human (and human-designed) agency. Prices, narratives, leverage, panic, and
patience are not only market properties — they are behavioural phenomena expressed through market
structure.

This volume is not a catalogue of every bias named in behavioural finance. It is a constitutional
investigation:

> What enduring properties of human behaviour shape investment decisions across time, culture, and
> market form — and which of those properties must any honest investment intelligence confront?

Cross-disciplinary convergence is sought among psychology, behavioural economics, trading practice,
neuroscience of decision-making (cautiously), anthropology of exchange, and market history.
Disagreement is preserved.

Each inquiry closes with:

| Element | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Established Observations** | Supported across multiple schools or by strong empirical pattern |
| **Competing Perspectives** | Where respected traditions genuinely disagree |
| **Candidate Constitutional Insights** | Tentative observations that may later qualify for elevation — not yet law |

## Inquiry 1 — What Kind of Agent Acts in Markets?

### The Question

Is the market participant a rational optimizer, a boundedly rational satisfier, a social imitator, a
narrative animal — or all of these under different conditions?

### Investigation

Classical finance often models agents as consistent expected-utility maximizers with stable
preferences. Behavioural research documents systematic departures: limited attention, framing
effects, loss aversion, overconfidence, and social influence. Practitioners describe fear and greed;
historians describe crowd behaviour; Herbert Simon described satisficing under cognitive limits.

Convergence is not on a single agent model. Convergence is on a more modest claim:

Humans in markets are **capacity-limited decision-makers** operating under incomplete information,
social influence, and emotional arousal — sometimes approximating optimization, often not.

An intelligence that assumes only the classical agent will misread both error and skill. An
intelligence that assumes only irrationality will miss genuine expertise and disciplined process.

### Established Observations

- Human market agents have limited attention, memory, and computational capacity.
- Behaviour systematically departs from textbook optimization in recurring ways.
- Some agents and institutions approximate disciplined process more closely than others; heterogeneity
  is real.

### Competing Perspectives

- **Homo economicus tradition:** Departures average out or are competed away by smarter money.
- **Behavioural tradition:** Departures are persistent, systematic, and often exploitable or harmful.
- **Adaptive markets tradition (e.g. Lo):** Rationality is ecological — heuristics can be fit or unfit
  depending on environment.
- **Institutional tradition:** The relevant "agent" is often an organization with incentives, not an
  individual psyche.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment intelligence must not assume a single universal agent model.
- Capacity limits and heterogeneity of agents are enduring features of market behaviour — not
  temporary bugs.

## Inquiry 2 — How Do Humans Perceive Gain and Loss?

### The Question

Do people evaluate outcomes as final wealth states — or as gains and losses relative to reference
points?

### Investigation

Prospect theory (Kahneman and Tversky) and a large subsequent literature find that people often
evaluate outcomes relative to reference points, weigh losses more heavily than equal gains, and
exhibit diminishing sensitivity to larger changes. Trading floors speak of "getting even"; investors
speak of "being underwater"; accountants and tax rules create additional reference frames.

Whether loss aversion is universal in strength is debated; that reference-dependent evaluation is
common is much less debated. Market history shows forced selling near pain thresholds and reluctance
to realize losses — patterns consistent with reference-dependent behaviour even when alternative
explanations (information, constraints, career risk) also apply.

### Established Observations

- Reference points strongly shape how outcomes are experienced and acted upon.
- Losses are often felt more intensely than equivalent gains (loss aversion as recurring pattern).
- The same economic outcome can produce opposite behaviour depending on framing and reference.

### Competing Perspectives

- Loss aversion is a deep preference vs. a context-dependent construct.
- Apparent loss aversion is sometimes rational response to leverage, taxes, or career incentives.
- Cultural variation in loss attitudes is material vs. secondary to a common core.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment reasoning that ignores reference points and loss asymmetry will mispredict behaviour —
  including the behaviour of the user it advises.
- "Rational" advice that is psychologically un-followable is incomplete advice.

## Inquiry 3 — What Is Overconfidence — and Why Does It Persist?

### The Question

Why do market participants so often overestimate their knowledge, forecasts, and control?

### Investigation

Overconfidence appears as miscalibration (too-narrow confidence intervals), better-than-average
effects, and illusion of control. It shows up in excessive trading, concentrated bets, and narrative
certainty. It persists because feedback in markets is noisy: lucky outcomes feel like skill; skill
can be punished by variance; social status rewards confident narratives.

Experts are not immune; in some domains expertise improves calibration, in others it increases
confident error. Tetlock's work on political judgment, trading research on overtrading, and
practitioner lore about "certainty before the fall" converge on persistence — not on identical
magnitude.

### Established Observations

- People frequently express more confidence than accuracy warrants.
- Noisy feedback environments (like markets) make calibration difficult.
- Confidence and competence are imperfectly coupled.

### Competing Perspectives

- Overconfidence is mainly individual bias vs. mainly incentive- and selection-driven (confident
  actors are promoted and heard).
- Markets punish overconfidence enough to limit its impact vs. not enough, especially with other
  people's money.
- Some "overconfidence" is functional for action under uncertainty.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Expressed confidence is not evidence of knowledge quality.
- An investment intelligence that mirrors user confidence without independent calibration criteria
  will amplify error.

## Inquiry 4 — How Does Attention Shape Markets?

### The Question

If attention is scarce, what happens to prices and decisions when attention concentrates or wanders?

### Investigation

Humans cannot monitor all securities, all news, or all risks. Attention is allocated. What is
attended becomes more likely to be traded, discussed, and priced; what is neglected can remain
mispriced longer — or simply untraded.

Barber and Odean and related work on attention-driven buying; news-media effects; meme-stock
coordination; and ordinary portfolio neglect of unwatched holdings all point to attention as a
binding constraint. Volume I's distributed knowledge meets Volume II's scarce attention: markets
aggregate information only through the narrow pipes of what agents notice.

### Established Observations

- Attention is scarce and selectively allocated.
- Attention shocks can move trading and prices independent of slow-moving fundamentals.
- Neglect is as important as focus; unseen risks are underweighted until they arrive.

### Competing Perspectives

- Attention effects are short-lived anomalies vs. persistent structural features.
- Professional capital largely corrects attention gaps vs. professionals share attention constraints
  and career narratives.
- Digital media changed the intensity of attention dynamics vs. merely accelerated old patterns.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- What is not attended cannot be reasoned about — for humans or for systems that inherit human
  question-framing.
- Investment intelligence must treat attention allocation as part of the decision problem, not as
  external to it.

## Inquiry 5 — How Do Emotions Enter Financial Decisions?

### The Question

Are emotions noise to be removed — or information and motivation that cannot be deleted without
deleting the decision-maker?

### Investigation

Fear, greed, regret, envy, hope, and shame appear throughout market history. Physiological arousal
changes risk-taking; stress narrows attention; mood affects optimism. Damasio's work on emotion in
decision-making suggests that "pure reason" without affective tagging is not how humans choose.
Trading psychology treats emotional regulation as skill; classical models often omit emotion
entirely.

Convergence: emotion is endogenous to market behaviour. Disagreement: whether emotion primarily
distorts or also enables decisive action under uncertainty; whether institutions can design around
emotion or must educate through it.

### Established Observations

- Emotional states measurably influence risk-taking and judgment.
- Extreme market episodes co-occur with extreme collective emotion.
- Attempts to ignore emotion in human decision systems do not eliminate it; they leave it
  unexamined.

### Competing Perspectives

- Emotion is mainly bias vs. partly adaptive signal (e.g. fear as risk alert).
- Best practice is emotional suppression vs. emotional awareness and process design.
- Algorithmic agents escape emotion vs. inherit the emotions of their designers and users through
  objectives and overrides.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Emotion is part of human market behaviour, not an optional overlay.
- An intelligence that advises humans must account for emotional follow-through — not only
  analytically correct recommendations.

## Inquiry 6 — How Does Social Influence Work in Markets?

### The Question

How much of investing is independent judgment — and how much is imitation, status, and belonging?

### Investigation

Humans copy. They copy success, copy proximity, copy prestige, and copy the crowd when uncertain.
Herding, information cascades, fashion in factors and narratives, and "nobody gets fired for buying
X" career herding are documented across economics, sociology, and practice.

Kindleberger's histories of manias are social as much as financial. Social media intensifies speed
and visibility of imitation but does not invent the underlying propensity.

Independent judgment exists — and is costly. Most participants, most of the time, lean on social
proof.

### Established Observations

- Imitation and social proof are recurring drivers of participation and positioning.
- Career and reputation incentives can induce herding even among professionals.
- Collective narratives coordinate action faster than private analysis alone.

### Competing Perspectives

- Herding is mostly rational Bayesian updating on others' information vs. mostly non-informational
  imitation.
- Crowds are wiser than individuals (condorcet-style) vs. crowds amplify error in markets.
- Social influence is stronger in retail than institutional settings vs. strong in both, differently
  expressed.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Market behaviour is partly social behaviour; models of isolated optimizers are incomplete.
- "Everyone knows" is a behavioural signal — sometimes information, sometimes synchronized risk.

## Inquiry 7 — How Do Narratives Shape Belief and Action?

### The Question

Do humans invest in cash flows — or in stories about cash flows?

### Investigation

Shiller and others emphasize narrative economics: stories spread like epidemics and shape
willingness to buy, hold, or panic. Practitioners know that a thesis is often a story with numbers
attached. Earnings are data; "the platform will own the market" is a narrative that organizes which
data count.

Narratives compress complexity, coordinate groups, and resist disconfirming evidence until a
breaking point. They are not merely lies; they are cognitive technology. The danger is when story
coherence is mistaken for predictive validity.

### Established Observations

- Narratives strongly influence which facts are noticed and how they are weighted.
- Competing narratives can coexist until price or event forces resolution.
- Story coherence is psychologically satisfying and epistemically dangerous when unchecked.

### Competing Perspectives

- Narratives are primarily causal drivers of markets vs. primarily epiphenomena of price and
  liquidity.
- Better investing means better stories vs. better investing means fewer stories and more process.
- Quantitative methods escape narrative vs. smuggle narrative into factor names and model choice.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment beliefs are often narrative-structured; evidence must be separable from story.
- An intelligence that cannot distinguish supporting data from narrative glue will confuse
  persuasion with reason.

## Inquiry 8 — What Is Time Preference — and Why Do Investors Struggle With Time?

### The Question

Why do humans chronically conflict with their own long-term plans in markets?

### Investigation

Hyperbolic discounting, present bias, and the emotional vividness of now versus the abstraction of
later appear in consumption and investment. Investors abandon long-term policy in drawdowns; they
chase recent performance; they prefer certain small gains to uncertain larger ones depending on
frame.

Retirement investing, compounding, and "sitting" on winners or losers are all time problems.
Volume I's uncertainty meets Volume II's impatience: the future is both unknown and underweighted.

### Established Observations

- Present outcomes weigh disproportionately relative to distant ones for many decision-makers.
- Stated long-term policy and revealed short-term behaviour frequently diverge.
- Market volatility interacts with time preference to produce policy abandonment.

### Competing Perspectives

- Present bias is irrational vs. partly rational under mortality, liquidity needs, or distrust of
  future self/institutions.
- Education fixes time inconsistency vs. only commitment devices and institutional design fix it.
- Professionals escape present bias vs. face quarterly evaluation versions of the same problem.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment intelligence must treat time inconsistency as expected human behaviour, not as rare
  failure.
- Advice that is optimal only for a perfectly patient agent is incomplete for real users.

## Inquiry 9 — How Do Humans Learn From Market Feedback?

### The Question

Do markets teach — or do they flatter, mislead, and occasionally educate?

### Investigation

Learning requires feedback. Market feedback is delayed, noisy, confounded by luck, and filtered by
survivorship. People attribute success to skill and failure to externality more often than
calibration requires. They learn rules of thumb from small samples. They overfit recent regimes.

Experts can learn — deliberate practice, base rates, checklists, and post-mortems help. But the
environment fights clean learning. This is why overconfidence persists (Inquiry 3) and why crashes
recur (Volume I): the lesson of fragility is learned, then slowly unlearned in calm.

### Established Observations

- Market feedback is noisy and easy to misattribute.
- Small-sample learning produces fragile beliefs.
- Structured review improves calibration relative to unstructured narrative memory.

### Competing Perspectives

- Markets are effective teachers for those who survive vs. survival itself biases the lesson.
- Statistical training largely corrects naive learning vs. deep biases remain after training.
- Skin in the game is necessary for real learning vs. insufficient without method.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Learning from markets requires method; experience alone does not guarantee wisdom.
- An intelligence that updates beliefs without guarding against noisy feedback will oscillate or
  overfit.

## Inquiry 10 — What Is Discipline — Behaviourally Speaking?

### The Question

If biases are common, what is the behavioural content of investment discipline?

### Investigation

Discipline is not the absence of emotion or bias. It is the presence of **precommitted process**:
rules for entry, exit, sizing, review, and refusal that bind the agent when arousal is high.

Checklists, investment policies, cooling-off periods, position limits, and "if X then Y" plans are
behavioural technologies. So are institutions that separate proposal from approval. Great investors
differ in philosophy but converge on respecting process under pressure — even when they disagree on
what the process should be.

### Established Observations

- Precommitment and process improve consistency relative to case-by-case improvisation under stress.
- Discipline is tested most when it is most needed — in pain, euphoria, and social pressure.
- Written policy and lived policy diverge without reinforcement mechanisms.

### Competing Perspectives

- Rigid rules outperform discretion vs. expert discretion outperforms rigid rules in some domains.
- Discipline is character vs. discipline is environment design.
- For AI systems, "discipline" is code vs. also includes when to refuse and when to escalate to
  humans.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Discipline is process that binds behaviour under emotional and social pressure — not mere
  intention.
- Investment intelligence should make its own process constraints explicit — including conditions for
  refusal.

## Inquiry 11 — Where Do Behavioural Patterns Meet Market Structure?

### The Question

Which Volume II patterns amplify Volume I's market properties?

### Investigation

Cross-volume convergence deserves special weight (CEB methodology):

| Market property (Vol I) | Behavioural amplifier (Vol II) |
| --- | --- |
| Price as signal, not oracle | Narrative and overconfidence treat price as verdict |
| Conditional liquidity | Fear and crowded exits withdraw liquidity together |
| Volatility ≠ full risk | Emotion equates drawdown with permanent loss |
| Risk vs uncertainty | Overconfidence converts uncertainty into false precision |
| Bubbles and crashes | Imitation, leverage psychology, and attention cascades |
| Disagreement clears markets | Social proof suppresses visible disagreement until breaks |

This is not a claim that behaviour "causes" all market phenomena. It is a claim that market nature
and human nature co-produce the patterns investment intelligence must respect.

### Established Observations

- Behavioural patterns and market structure interact; neither is a complete explanation alone.
- Stress regimes synchronize behaviour and degrade liquidity and calibration together.

### Competing Perspectives

- Behavioural explanation is primary vs. institutional/credit explanation is primary vs. both are
  necessary at different scales.
- Micro bias aggregation is enough to explain macro patterns vs. macro patterns require additional
  mechanisms.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment intelligence foundations must integrate market nature and human behaviour — not stack
  them as unrelated modules.
- The most durable principles may be those where Volume I and Volume II independently converge.

## Inquiry 12 — What Must Investment Intelligence Assume About Humans?

### The Question

Before recommending, what behavioural truths must an honest system treat as default?

### Investigation

Synthesizing the inquiries: a system that advises humans about markets should default to assuming —

1. Limited attention and capacity
2. Reference-dependent evaluation of gains and losses
3. Imperfect calibration of confidence
4. Emotional influence under uncertainty
5. Social and narrative influence on belief
6. Present bias relative to stated long-term aims
7. Noisy learning from experience
8. Need for process constraints under pressure
9. Heterogeneity — not everyone exhibits every pattern equally
10. Dignity — users are not merely error terms; they are agents with goals and constraints

These are descriptive defaults for design humility — not insults, and not constitutional law.

### Established Observations

- Systems designed only for ideal agents fail real users in predictable ways.
- Heterogeneity means population averages do not dictate every individual interaction.
- Respect for agency and honesty about limits can coexist.

### Competing Perspectives

- How strongly to "nudge" vs. inform vs. defer is ethically and practically contested.
- Whether AI advisors should expose behavioural framing or avoid paternalism is unresolved.
- Cultural variation may require local calibration of behavioural defaults.

### Candidate Constitutional Insights

- Investment intelligence should assume capacity-limited, socially influenced, emotionally engaged
  agents unless evidence shows otherwise in context.
- Refusal, uncertainty disclosure, and process clarity are behavioural necessities — not optional
  polish.

## Volume Synthesis — Candidate Insights for Later Review

Candidates only — not GAR-0021 text:

1. Market agents are capacity-limited and heterogeneous; no single rationality model suffices.
2. Reference points and loss asymmetry shape behaviour; ignore them and advice becomes un-followable.
3. Confidence is not calibration; mirroring confidence amplifies error.
4. Attention is scarce; neglected risks and hyped narratives are first-class decision facts.
5. Emotion is endogenous to market decisions; unexamined emotion becomes unmanaged risk.
6. Social imitation and career herding are recurring coordination mechanisms — and synchronized risk.
7. Narratives organize belief; data and story must be separable.
8. Time inconsistency predictably breaks long-term policy under volatility.
9. Market feedback teaches poorly without method; experience ≠ wisdom.
10. Discipline is precommitted process under pressure — including refusal conditions.
11. Volume I market properties and Volume II behavioural patterns co-produce fragility and opportunity.
12. Honest investment intelligence defaults to human limits without denying human agency.

history. Individual named biases are treated as instances of deeper patterns (attention, reference,

## Closing

Volume II does not claim that humans are broken.

It claims something stricter:

> Before an intelligence recommends, it must demonstrate that it understands the kind of mind that
> will receive the recommendation — including limits, emotions, social nature, and capacity for
> discipline.

Markets are human processes.
Investment intelligence that forgets the human forgets the market.

Discover before defining.
Reason before Recommendation.
